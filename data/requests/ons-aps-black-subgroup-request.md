# Request for APS Labour Market Rates by Detailed Black Ethnic Group

## Background

The Annual Population Survey (APS) published via Nomis provides labour-market rates (employment, unemployment, economic inactivity) by ethnicity. However, the published tables only expose a broad **"Black or Black British"** aggregate. They do not separately report rates for:

- Black African
- Black Caribbean
- Other Black

This aggregation masks significant differences between subgroups with distinct migration histories, settlement patterns, and labour-market outcomes.

## Specific request

We are requesting a **commissioned table** from the APS with the following specification:

### Variables

| Variable | Detail requested |
|---|---|
| **Employment rate** | 16–64, percentage |
| **Unemployment rate** | 16+, percentage |
| **Economic inactivity rate** | 16–64, percentage |

### Ethnic group disaggregation

- Black African
- Black Caribbean
- Other Black
- All Black (derived aggregate)
- All ethnicities (baseline comparison)

### Geography

- United Kingdom (national)
- Optionally: 9 English regions + Wales + Scotland (if sample sizes permit)

### Time period

- Latest available rolling annual estimate (e.g., Oct 2024 – Sep 2025)

### Additional fields

- Confidence intervals (95% CI)
- Sample sizes (unweighted count)

## Rationale

The UK Black Demographics platform uses official statistics to present a detailed picture of Black populations in the United Kingdom. Employment and economic activity are among the most-visited sections. Currently, we can only show a single aggregate "Black" rate, which is insufficient for:

1. **Policy analysis** — Black Caribbean and Black African communities have different employment patterns
2. **Community understanding** — visitors to the site expect subgroup detail consistent with other sections (education, health, housing)
3. **Academic research** — researchers require subgroup disaggregation for valid analysis

Other official sources (DfE exclusions, EFF home ownership, NHS Mental Health Act data) already publish these subgroups. The APS sample is large enough to support this disaggregation at national level.
